Education

What is somatic bodywork?

Somatic comes from the Greek soma — meaning the body, as lived. It's the difference between massaging muscles and listening to a nervous system. Here's what makes this work different.

01

It's a conversation, not a procedure.

Traditional massage works on you. Somatic work works with you. I'll ask what you notice, where your attention goes, what feels stuck. The body has been waiting to be asked.

02

It treats the nervous system, not just the muscle.

Chronic pain and tension often live downstream of a nervous system stuck in alert mode. We work with the vagus nerve, breath, and gentle touch to bring the body back toward rest.

03

It honors what's been stored.

Old grief, old fear, old vigilance — your tissue remembers all of it. Somatic work creates a safe space for those patterns to release at the pace your body chooses, never forced.

When the pain isn't just the pain.

Most chronic pain has more than one address. There's the spot that hurts — and there's the nervous system that hasn't been able to put down its guard. Working with one without the other only gets you so far.

I see this with people carrying:

  • Tension that physical therapy hasn't fully resolved
  • Old injuries the body is still bracing around
  • Anxiety, hypervigilance, or a sense of never truly resting
  • Disconnection from the body after trauma or loss
  • Headaches, jaw pain, gut issues that come and go
  • Sleep that won't quite arrive, or won't stay

If any of that sounds familiar, this work is for you. Start with a Trauma Somatic Healing Session — we'll go from there.

Questions

What you might be wondering.

I've never had somatic work before. What should I expect?

We'll start with a short conversation — what brought you in, what your body's been holding, what you'd like to feel afterward. Then you'll lie down on a heated table, fully clothed or undraped depending on the modality, and I'll work slowly. You can talk, stay quiet, ask questions, or fall asleep. There's no wrong way to be in the room.

What's the difference between this and a regular massage?

A regular massage moves muscles. Somatic work also listens to the nervous system, the breath, the places that flinch or soften. The pressure is often lighter than people expect — the goal isn't to push through tension, it's to invite it to release. Many clients find this work goes deeper, with less force, than anything they've tried.

Can cryotherapy actually help with chronic pain?

Cold has been used for pain since Hippocrates wrote about it. Research today shows targeted cryotherapy can reduce inflammation, calm nerve sensitivity, and offer real relief for ongoing pain — especially when paired with nervous system work. I usually recommend a series of six sessions for chronic conditions, with maintenance after.

Is this work safe if I'm dealing with trauma or PTSD?

This work is built for it. Every session is paced to your nervous system, not the clock. I never push past what your body is ready for. That said, I'm a somatic practitioner — not a licensed mental health provider. If you're in active crisis or working through significant trauma, please also stay connected with a therapist or doctor. We can complement that care beautifully.

How often should I come in?

It depends what you're working with. For chronic pain or cryotherapy series, weekly for six sessions tends to land the best results. For somatic and nervous system work, every two to four weeks builds steady regulation. Many clients land in a monthly rhythm once their baseline shifts. We'll figure out what fits your body and your life.

Do you serve men, or just women?

Most of my practice is women, but men are welcome. The work is the same — the only requirement is that you arrive willing to slow down.

A note on this work: Sessions at LumenHeart are designed to support your overall wellbeing and complement medical or psychological care — they do not diagnose, treat, or cure medical or psychiatric conditions. Results vary, and depend on your unique body, history, and consistency. Please consult your physician before beginning any new wellness practice, especially if you have a medical condition, are pregnant, or are managing chronic pain under medical care.
